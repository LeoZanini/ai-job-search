## Overview
Leonardo's behavioral pattern centers on **adaptability and cross-disciplinary bridging**. He moved from mechanical engineering into self-taught full-stack development, then into product strategy and B2B sales, picking up each domain by doing it under real pressure. He thrives in small, high-autonomy teams where he can shape the solution end-to-end, but explicitly says he'll flex into larger, more structured environments when needed for income stability.

## Core Behavioral Drives

| Drive | Level | Meaning |

## Strongest Behaviors
- **Rapid domain-switching:** Learned full-stack web development, AI agent tooling, and B2B sales largely on the job, on top of an unrelated mechanical engineering degree.
- **Multidisciplinary bridge-building:** Understands both the code and the client/business side (consultative sales at Kodama, product strategy at Connfit) instead of treating engineering as isolated from the customer problem.
- **Active feedback-seeking:** Explicitly wants direct, frequent feedback at this stage of his career and says he won't lose humility over it - a strong signal for mentorship-heavy environments.

## How You Work Best
- Small teams / high-autonomy environments where you can own a solution end-to-end
- Short, visible delivery cycles (comfortable with longer cycles too, just prefers short)
- Frequent, direct feedback loops, especially from more senior engineers
- Execution-first role for now, to build depth before returning to more architectural/leadership responsibility

## Growth Areas (frame positively in applications)

- **Broader exposure to software architecture patterns:** Frame as a developer who has shipped real production systems solo/co-founder-style and is now seeking a team environment with more senior engineers to learn architecture patterns beyond what self-teaching covered.

## Mapping to Job Posting Language

When a job posting mentions these keywords, it's a **strong behavioral fit**:
- "fast-paced", "own end-to-end", "small team", "wear many hats"
- "junior developer with room to grow", "mentorship available", "feedback culture"
- "cross-functional", "work directly with founders/product"

When a job posting mentions these, flag as **potential friction** (not a deal-breaker - he says he'll adapt for the right pay/stability):
- "rigid, long release cycles", "strict single-responsibility scope with no room for input"
- "heavy process/bureaucracy" without mention of growth or mentorship

## Management Style Preferences
- Works best with managers/senior engineers who give direct, frequent feedback and are willing to explain the "why" behind architectural decisions.
- Needs some visible path to more ownership over time - a purely narrow, static scope with zero room to contribute ideas would eventually be draining, though he can tolerate it short-term for stability.

## Using This in Applications
- **Cover letters:** Lead with the adaptability narrative (mechanical engineering -> self-taught full-stack -> product/sales) as evidence of fast learning, not as a liability. Emphasize eagerness for feedback and mentorship rather than claiming senior-level independence.
- **CV:** Emphasize the breadth of what he owned solo/as co-founder (full stack, product, sales) as evidence of range, while being honest that this was outside a traditional "employee with a manager" structure.
- **Interviews:** Use STAR examples that show fast ramp-up in a new domain (e.g., rebuilding the Connfit scheduling engine, teaching an AI curriculum from a non-CS background) and comfort taking direction/feedback.
- **Don't overstate:** Avoid presenting himself as an experienced architect or senior engineer - frame as a fast-learning, adaptable junior who has already shipped real production systems and wants a team to grow further in.
